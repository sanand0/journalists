# 60 Lakh in Limbo: Bengal's Voter Roll Revision Creates an Electoral Category India Has Never Seen

> The Supreme Court invoked extraordinary powers to publish a voter list with millions of names marked 'under adjudication' --- a status that exists in no Indian statute. The burden falls overwhelmingly on Muslim-majority border districts.

_For editorial review --- Draft dated 2 March 2026 \| Not for publication_

When West Bengal's Chief Electoral Officer published the state's final voter roll on 28 February 2026, it contained something no Indian electoral roll has carried before: a third category. Alongside the 6.44 crore voters marked "approved" and the 61.7 lakh names deleted, an additional **60,06,675 citizens** --- roughly 8.5% of the pre-revision electorate --- were listed as "under adjudication."

These voters are neither enrolled nor removed. They cannot cast ballots until individual judicial officers clear their names through supplementary lists that have no fixed deadline. The term "under adjudication" appears in no provision of the Representation of the People Act 1950, the Registration of Electors Rules 1960, or the Electoral Roll Management Rules 2003. It was created entirely through a Supreme Court order invoking Article 142 --- the Constitution's extraordinary "complete justice" power --- to manage a backlog that the existing statutory machinery could not clear in time.

With assembly elections expected within months, the exercise has produced three outcomes that analysts and legal experts say have no documented precedent in Indian electoral history: judicial officers borrowed from neighbouring Jharkhand and Odisha to adjudicate voter disputes, a state cabinet announcing compensation for deaths attributed to the revision's pressure, and a published roll carrying a suspended-verification category for millions. Together, they amount to what the Association for Democratic Reforms (ADR), a non-partisan election watchdog and petitioner before the Supreme Court, has called "an unprecedented administrative experiment on the electorate."

## The exercise: what the numbers show

The Special Intensive Revision (SIR) --- a house-to-house re-verification of every voter in the state --- was ordered on 27 October 2025 by the Election Commission of India (ECI) as Phase II of a nationwide initiative, with a qualifying date of 1 January 2026. West Bengal's pre-revision electorate stood at approximately 7.66 crore. By the time the final roll was published, it had shrunk to 7.04 crore --- a net reduction of roughly 62 lakh names, according to CEO West Bengal's press note (CEO/PN-05/2026).

The deletions fell into categories that are routine in roll maintenance: 24.17 lakh deceased voters, 19.88 lakh who had shifted residence, 12.20 lakh found absent on repeated visits, and 1.38 lakh duplicates. What was not routine was the residual: 60.07 lakh names flagged by the ECI's automated "logical discrepancy" algorithm as requiring further verification --- too many to resolve before the court-mandated publication deadline, and too consequential to simply delete.

The CEO's press note classified the final roll into three explicit categories --- Approved, Deleted, and Under Adjudication --- and stated that names cleared by judicial officers would be added through supplementary lists. Business Today reported this categorisation as a first in Indian electoral history.

## A status the law does not recognise

India's electoral framework, as enacted by Parliament, contemplates a binary: a citizen's name is either on the roll or it is not. Section 62 of the Representation of the People Act 1951 grants voting rights to "every person who is entered in the electoral roll" --- no qualifier exists for conditional or suspended entry.

Article 326 of the Constitution lists exactly four grounds for disqualification from voter registration: non-residence, unsoundness of mind, crime, and corrupt practice. "Pending adjudication of logical discrepancy" is not among them.

The Supreme Court's workaround, fashioned in its 24 February 2026 order in W.P.(C) No. 1089/2025 (ADR v. ECI) and connected matters, used Article 142 to "deem" future supplementary lists as part of the final roll published on 28 February. This retroactive deeming provision was designed to prevent outright disenfranchisement once a voter is positively cleared. But it leaves unanswered the critical question: what happens to a voter who remains "under adjudication" on polling day?

No publicly available ECI circular or presiding officer instruction addresses this scenario. The statutory appeals mechanism under Section 24 of the RPA 1950 --- which routes disputes from the Electoral Registration Officer to the District Magistrate and then to the CEO --- was effectively bypassed in favour of a court-directed adjudicatory structure operating outside the normal chain.

## Judges borrowed across state lines: a first

The scale of the backlog forced a procedural invention. The Calcutta High Court reported to the Supreme Court that its 250 available civil judges could process approximately 250 cases per day each --- meaning the 80-lakh-odd pending cases would require roughly 80 working days, far beyond the 28 February deadline.

On 24 February, the Supreme Court bench of Chief Justice Surya Kant, Justice Bagchi, and Justice Pancholi authorised the Chief Justice of the Calcutta High Court to requisition serving or former judicial officers from Jharkhand and Odisha. Approximately 530 judicial officers from three states were eventually deployed, with the ECI bearing travel, boarding, and honorarium costs.

The closest historical parallel is Assam's Foreigners' Tribunals, which adjudicate "D-voter" (doubtful voter) cases in the context of the National Register of Citizens. But those tribunals operate within a single state under a specific statute --- the Foreigners Act, 1946 --- and address citizenship rather than voter registration. No prior voter roll revision in any Indian state appears to have required borrowing judges across state boundaries, based on available case law and election records.

## Deaths, compensation, and contested causation

At least eight Booth Level Officers (BLOs) --- the school teachers and local government staff who form the frontline of voter enumeration --- had died in West Bengal by 11 January 2026, according to Outlook India. Al Jazeera documented at least 33 BLO deaths nationwide since the SIR began on 4 November, with at least nine confirmed suicides.

Chief Minister Mamata Banerjee announced Rs 2 lakh ex-gratia compensation for families of 39 persons she described as victims of "SIR stress." The state subsequently offered home-guard jobs to next of kin of 56 people, including 18 BLOs, who died during the SIR period, as reported by the Times of India. The TMC later raised its claimed death toll to 120 by 28 February 2026. Whether the compensation was formalised through a gazetted cabinet order or remained an executive announcement could not be independently verified from available records.

No comparable state-cabinet compensation decision tied to a voter roll revision was found in the research materials reviewed for this report.

**A critical caveat on death attribution:** At least one prominently cited BLO death --- Hamimul Islam, 47, found hanging in a Murshidabad school on 10 January 2026 --- was subsequently attributed by police to harassment over an unpaid Rs 20 lakh personal loan. A TMC worker was arrested for abetting his suicide. The TMC had staged protests linking this death to the SIR before the police findings emerged --- a pattern that warrants caution in accepting aggregate death-toll claims at face value.

## Where the burden falls: a geographic pattern

District-level data, compiled from PTI and IANS wire reports citing CEO West Bengal office officials, reveals a pronounced geographic concentration. The five districts with the highest "under adjudication" numbers all have Muslim populations above 25% (per the 2011 Census, the most recent available) and share borders with Bangladesh.

The three Muslim-majority or near-majority districts alone --- Murshidabad, Malda, and Uttar Dinajpur --- account for approximately 24.09 lakh cases, or roughly 40% of all "under adjudication" entries, despite comprising an estimated 18% of the state's electorate. At the bottom of the table, Kalimpong (0.93% Muslim) and Jhargram (8.31% Muslim) carry fewer than 7,000 cases each.

A Times of India report, citing analysis by the Sabar Institute, found that Muslim names were disproportionately represented on the "logical discrepancy" list in five North Kolkata constituencies relative to their population share.

However, the pattern is not reducible to a single communal narrative. The Matua (Namasudra Scheduled Caste) Hindu refugee community in the BJP-leaning Nadia-North 24 Parganas belt was also heavily affected. Tribune India reported Matua leaders claiming "nearly 90% of the community has been hit." The SIR's "legacy linkage" requirement --- tracing voter names to the 2002 electoral roll --- structurally burdens anyone who migrated after that date, whether Muslim families in Murshidabad or Hindu refugees who settled from Bangladesh in recent decades.

**The Bhabanipur--Nandigram contrast** has drawn political attention: CM Banerjee's Bhabanipur constituency reportedly saw 47,094 deletions and over 14,000 names under adjudication, while BJP Leader of Opposition Suvendu Adhikari's Nandigram recorded a net addition of 770 voters. This comparison rests on two data points and may reflect genuine demographic differences between urban Kolkata and rural East Midnapore, but it warrants systematic constituency-level investigation.

## On the ground: shifting rules, overwhelmed workers, angry voters

The ECI's own affidavit to the Supreme Court documented what it called a process "crippled by threats and violence." According to Bar and Bench, the legal news outlet that reported on the filing, the affidavit recorded a sitting minister threatening to "break the legs of the Election Commission," burning of Form 7 objection documents, an attack on the Special Roll Observer's vehicle in South 24 Parganas, and "pervasive reluctance" by state police to register complaints from BLOs. The West Bengal CEO was granted Y-category security --- making him, by available records, the only electoral official in India with such protection.

Documentation requirements shifted repeatedly during the exercise. The ECI initially prescribed 13 identity proofs but notably excluded Aadhaar, voter ID (EPIC), ration cards, and PAN cards. Aadhaar was added only after Supreme Court intervention on 8 September 2025. Madhyamik (Class 10) admit cards were included from 19 January 2026. The Reporters' Collective, an investigative outlet, documented a pattern of rapidly changing instructions disseminated through informal channels such as WhatsApp, with local officials struggling to keep up with software-driven workflow changes.

In Farakka (Murshidabad), approximately 30 BLOs withdrew from SIR duties on 14 January 2026, citing workload pressure, procedural confusion, and rising voter hostility, as reported by The Federal. On the same day, approximately 1,100 villagers stormed a hearing camp at the Block Development Officer's office, demanding that hearings be conducted at local polling booths rather than requiring travel to distant centres.

That the automated "logical discrepancy" criteria could ensnare even the most well-documented citizens was illustrated when Nobel laureate Amartya Sen was reportedly flagged --- because the algorithm calculated an age difference of fewer than 15 years between him and his recorded parents.

## The courts: intervention without resolution

The Supreme Court's engagement has been extensive but has not resolved the fundamental tension. ADR filed W.P.(C) No. 640/2025 through advocate Prashant Bhushan, arguing the SIR violated Articles 14, 19, 21, 325, and 326. CM Banerjee took the extraordinary step of personally filing and arguing an Article 32 petition (W.P.(C) No. 129/2026). TMC MPs Mahua Moitra and Derek O'Brien filed separate pleas, as did Yogendra Yadav of Bharat Jodo Abhiyaan.

The CJI acknowledged a "trust deficit" between the state government and the ECI --- a candid judicial observation that the existing institutional framework had broken down. Yet the Court declined to stay the process, instead fashioning the supplementary-list mechanism as a middle path: the roll would be published on schedule, and adjudication would continue under judicial supervision.

A joint convention of the People's Union for Civil Liberties (PUCL) and the National Alliance of People's Movements (NAPM), convened on 20 December 2025, produced a jury report finding that the SIR imposed "unreasonable burden on ordinary citizens, particularly migrants, the poor, and those without stable housing."

## How other states handled the SIR

West Bengal was not the only state to undergo SIR in this cycle, but it was the only one to produce this combination of outcomes. Tamil Nadu's final SIR list, also published in late February 2026, reported 97.37 lakh deletions --- but used the standard binary framework of additions and deletions with no "under adjudication" category, according to the Economic Times. Bihar, which underwent Phase I of the SIR, similarly completed the exercise without requiring judicial cross-deputation or producing a third roll category.

The Assam NRC experience, while operating under a different statute (the Foreigners Act rather than the RPA), offers the closest structural parallel: a legacy-linkage requirement, algorithmic flagging of discrepancies, mass hearings before quasi-judicial officers, and a disproportionate impact on specific communities. The Outlook India analysis drew this comparison explicitly, noting that the SIR's "legacy linkage to 2002 rolls mirrors the Assam NRC's requirement to trace ancestry to 1971 voter lists."

## What happens next --- and why it matters beyond Bengal

The immediate question is arithmetic. With 60.07 lakh cases, 530 judicial officers processing at approximately 250 cases per day, and assembly elections expected in April--May 2026, clearance would require roughly 45 working days under ideal conditions --- assuming no disruptions, no appeals, and full officer availability. Whether hearings before civil judges from Jharkhand and Odisha, who may not speak Bengali, constitute the "reasonable opportunity of being heard" that Section 22 of the RPA 1950 requires is a question no court has yet addressed.

The deeper concern is precedential. The SIR has demonstrated that administrative deadlines can override adjudication completion, with courts using Article 142 to patch legitimacy after the fact. If the "under adjudication" mechanism is normalised --- whether in future West Bengal exercises or in other states --- it introduces the possibility that millions of voters could be placed in a legal limbo through algorithmic flags and bureaucratic delay, with no statutory framework governing their rights or the timeline for resolution.

The ECI has not published the algorithm's specifications. ADR's request for machine-readable electoral data was met with Supreme Court reservations about privacy. The criteria that placed 60 lakh citizens in an unprecedented category remain, for now, a black box.
