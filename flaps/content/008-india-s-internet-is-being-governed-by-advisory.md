## India’s Internet is Being Governed by Advisory

**Headline**
**India’s Internet May Soon Be Run by “Advisories” With Legal Teeth**

**Deck**
The bigger story is not one more takedown rule. It is a new way of governing the internet: guidance that used to be optional may now become enforceable. ([Reuters][1])

**Intro**
Everyone notices the visible fights — deepfakes, AI misuse, illegal content, platform liability. But the deeper shift is procedural. India has proposed changes to its IT rules that would make government advisories and clarifications legally binding on platforms such as Meta, Google and X. Non-compliance could count as failure to meet safe-harbour conditions, the legal shield that protects platforms from liability for user posts. ([Reuters][1])

**Subhead**
**Guidance Is Turning Into Obligation**

Until now, advisories on matters such as deepfake labelling and content takedown practices functioned more like guidance. Under the proposed change, they would carry consequences. That is the real shift: not just stricter rules, but a faster and more executive-led way of making them matter. ([Reuters][1])

**Subhead**
**The Internet Rulebook Is Speeding Up**

This follows a year in which the government already compressed the takedown window for flagged content to three hours from 36, and tightened obligations around AI-generated content and deepfakes. Put differently, India is not just regulating platforms more. It is trying to regulate them faster. ([Reuters][1])

**Subhead**
**Why That Matters**

Formal laws are slow, but clear. Advisories are quicker, but blurrier. That gives the state agility in a fast-changing online world. It also creates a new uncertainty for platforms, users and smaller firms: where exactly does policy end and law begin? The ministry says the proposal is meant to “strengthen enforceability” and “improve legal certainty.” Those two goals may not always feel identical to the people being governed by them. ([Reuters][1])

**Proof box**
**4 numbers / facts that make the case**

- **3 hours**: New takedown timeline for flagged content, down from 36 hours. ([Reuters][1])

- **AI and deepfake obligations already added**: This is part of a broader tightening, not a one-off move. ([Reuters][1])
- **April 14**: Deadline for public feedback on the proposal. ([Reuters][1])
