## India Wants Less China. Its Factories Want More.

**Headline**
**India Is Blocking China at the Border — and Buying It in the Boiler Room**

**Deck**
The visible story is restriction: CCTV bans, tighter tech scrutiny, security worries. The deeper story is dependence: Indian factories still rely on Chinese components, machinery and chemical intermediates in the sectors that matter most. ([The Times of India][6])

**Standfirst / opening copy**
In one direction, India is pushing China out. In the other, it is quietly letting China back in. This week, Chinese surveillance giants Hikvision and Dahua were effectively shut out of India’s internet-connected CCTV market as the government refused certification for products made in China or using Chinese chipsets. Days earlier, India relaxed procurement rules for some state-run firms so they could source critical equipment from China. Earlier this month, it also approved limited easing of Chinese investment curbs. It looks contradictory only until you see where the dependence actually sits. ([The Times of India][6])

**Section header**: **The Problem Is Not in the Mall. It Is in the Machine Room.**

If China were just a seller of cheap finished goods, decoupling would be painful but manageable. That is not the Indian reality. China sits deeper in the stack — inside components, machine tools, telecom gear, electronics hardware and drug intermediates. An official parliamentary reply shows China accounted for **15.74%** of India’s total imports in FY25. That sounds large, but not overwhelming. The shock comes when you look category by category.

**Section header**: **The “Overall” Number Hides the Real Dependence**

In FY25, China supplied **37.94%** of India’s electronics components imports, **43.41%** of telecom instruments, **49.46%** of computer hardware and peripherals, **45.30%** of industrial machinery for dairy etc., and **74.11%** of bulk drugs and drug intermediates. That is the story. India is not broadly dependent on China in some vague geopolitical sense. It is specifically dependent where factories, hospitals and infrastructure projects cannot easily improvise.

**Section header**: **This Is Why Restrictions and Relaxations Can Coexist**

Once that becomes clear, the policy pattern makes more sense. India can block Chinese-origin CCTV on security grounds and still reopen access for certain critical equipment. It can tighten on connected hardware while loosening on investment and procurement where domestic capacity is still too thin. What looks like inconsistency is really triage. India is trying to reduce strategic exposure without stalling industrial activity. ([The Times of India][6])

**Section header**: **China+1 Still Has a Lot of China in It**

The electronics numbers show how hard this unwind will be. NITI Aayog’s Trade Watch says India exported **$42.1 billion** of electronics in 2024 but imported **$100.6 billion**, leaving a **$58.5 billion** deficit. It also notes that exports are heavily concentrated in mobile-phone assembly, while imports remain concentrated in core components such as integrated circuits, displays and semiconductors. India is growing in electronics. But it is still growing around a deep imported core.

**Proof box**
**6 numbers that expose the contradiction**

- **15.74%**: China’s share of India’s total imports in FY25.
- **37.94%**: China’s share in India’s electronics-components imports.
- **43.41%**: China’s share in telecom instruments.
- **49.46%**: China’s share in computer hardware and peripherals.
- **74.11%**: China’s share in bulk drugs and drug intermediates.
- **$58.5bn**: India’s electronics trade deficit in 2024.
