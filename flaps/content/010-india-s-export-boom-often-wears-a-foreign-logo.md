## India’s Export Boom Often Wears A Foreign Logo

**Headline**
**India’s New Export Engine Often Does Not Carry an Indian Brand**

**Deck**
A growing share of India’s services strength is no longer flowing only through classic Indian IT firms. It is being built inside foreign multinationals’ India centres. ([Reuters][3])

**Intro**
When Indians hear “services exports,” many still picture the old icons: Indian IT majors, outsourcing, back-office support. That picture is dated. India’s services exports are estimated at **$348.4 billion** for April–January FY26, and the sector’s export share in GDP has risen to **10%** in the first half of FY26. At the same time, global capability centres, or GCCs, are no longer just low-cost support shops. India now hosts **1,700+ GCCs**, employing **1.9 million** people and generating **$64.6 billion** in revenue as of 2024. ([Press Information Bureau][4])

**Subhead**
**The Work Is Indian. The Logo Often Is Not**

That is the shift. A larger slice of India’s high-value work is happening inside foreign companies’ India operations. Reuters reports that Revolut plans to place around **40%** of its global workforce in India by end-2026, taking its India headcount to **5,500**, with work spanning product development, payment processing, fraud investigations and AI-based alerts. India is not just selling labour anymore. It is increasingly running important chunks of other countries’ firms. ([Reuters][3])

**Subhead**
**These Centres Are Moving Up the Org Chart**

The strongest evidence is not just hiring. It is authority. Economic Times reports that nearly **20%** of India’s GCCs now wield real strategic authority, up from **5%** a decade ago. India-based global leadership roles have grown to **6,500+** from just **115** in 2015, with projections of **30,000** by 2030. This is the deeper story: India is exporting not just tasks, but judgment. ([The Economic Times][5])

**Subhead**
**Why This Matters**

This changes how India should think about its own rise. The work is real. The value creation is real. The talent ladder is real. But the prestige accounting is weird: India wins, while someone else’s brand gets the credit. That is not a small detail. It changes cities, salaries, office markets, state competition, and the skills that become most valuable. It also means India’s next export boom may be harder to see if you are looking only for Indian company names. ([Reuters][3])
