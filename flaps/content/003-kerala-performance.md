## Kerala Performance

**Headline**
**Kerala’s Election Is No Longer Just About Welfare. It Is About Ageing**

**Deck**
Kerala has become the first Indian state to present an Elderly Budget. That turns ageing from a social fact into the organising principle of public finance. ([Niyama Sabha][9])

**Intro**
Kerala’s 2026-27 budget quietly reframes the state’s whole political economy. Senior citizens already account for **18.7%** of Kerala’s population, and that share is projected to rise to **22.8% by 2036**. In response, the state has presented India’s first **Elderly Budget**, with a consolidated outlay of **₹46,236.52 crore**, or **19.07%** of the total budget. ([Niyama Sabha][9])

**Subhead**: **This Is Not a Welfare Add-On. It Is a New Budget Logic**

That is the real story. Kerala is no longer just spending on welfare in the old sense. It is beginning to budget for an ageing society as a system: pensions, health care, home-based services, care infrastructure, retirement housing, and the “silver economy.” Even local-body fund devolution is now being linked to the proportion of senior citizens and urbanisation. ([Onmanorama][10])

**Subhead**: **But One Hard Truth Sits Inside the Numbers**

The Budget is visionary, but it is also revealing. A large share of this elderly allocation is not new discretionary care spending but pension commitments already embedded in the state’s obligations. Business Standard reported that about **68%** of the elderly budget is accounted for by retired government employees’ pensions. So the flap is not merely “Kerala cares for the old.” It is: Kerala is the first state where ageing is visibly becoming a fiscal structure. ([Business Standard][11])

**Subhead**: **That Changes the Election Question**

The usual Kerala frame is ideology, welfare, and migration income. The sharper frame now is this: can Kerala build a real care economy before ageing turns into a fiscal squeeze? That means asking not just about pensions, but about chronic care, mobility, housing, loneliness, local services, and the jobs that an ageing society creates. ([Onmanorama][10])

**Proof box**
**5 numbers that make the case**

- **18.7%** of Kerala’s population is aged 60+ in 2026. ([Niyama Sabha][9])
- **22.8%** projected share by 2036. ([Niyama Sabha][9])
- **₹46,236.52 crore** elderly-budget outlay. ([Niyama Sabha][9])
- **19.07%** of the total state budget is tagged to ageing. ([Niyama Sabha][9])
- **68%** of the elderly budget is projected to be pension-driven. ([Business Standard][11])
