```markdown
Research 10 Times of India flaps.
Avoid content already covered by the Times of India (or other mainstream media). Be original.
List them all and evaluate them on the tests that matter. Pick the 3 best ones and write them out.

## What Is a Flap?

A flap is not a pretty poster with data. It is a **one-page argument**.

It takes a messy, noisy topic and finds the one sharp idea hiding inside it — then proves that idea from multiple angles using numbers, comparisons, history, and one or two human details that make it land.

The Iran flap is not "war news." It's "here is exactly how this war hits India — through flights, remittances, markets, oil, and 90 lakh lives in the Gulf." The Japan flap is not "about Japan." It's "low growth may not equal failure, and your country is probably next." The Pakistan oil flap is not "energy story." It's a debunking.

Every flap is trying to make the reader feel, in under two minutes: _I thought I knew what this was about. I didn't._

## The News Hook Is Just the Door

Start with something happening _right now_ that your reader already has an opinion about. But walk through it into the room behind — where the 20-year story lives.

**The move is always one of these:**

- "This is not what you think."
- "The real story is elsewhere."
- "The numbers say something unexpected."
- "This matters to you more than it seems."

The best flaps are **contrarian without being clickbait**. They don't merely repeat the obvious news line. They widen it, overturn it, or pin it down.

## How to Research: Find the Friction

Don't gather facts. Find the _friction_ — numbers that surprise, contrast, or reveal a hidden truth.

**Three types of data every flap needs:**

1. **The "Whoa" Number** — one stat that stops you cold. Then translate it into human terms. Don't say "per capita electricity grew 70x." Say "in 1950 it was 18 units — what a 1-tonne AC burns in 18 hours."

2. **The Long Arc** — go back 20, 30, 50 years. Show how we got here. Readers trust graphs that start in 1950 more than graphs that start last Tuesday.

3. **The Comparison** — India vs world, then vs now, promise vs reality. Comparison is what calibrates. Is 2.6% unemployment good or bad? You don't know until Japan sits next to the US.

Before design starts, lock these things: _What is the one-sentence argument? What is the reader secretly wondering? What are 4–6 proof points that build the case — not random facts, but evidence? And who feels this in real life?_

## How to Write

**Headlines are conclusions, not labels.**

| Don't write               | Write instead                                          |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| India's Energy Dependence | Source of 50% of Oil, 80% of Gas                       |
| Data on Passports         | Half the World Is Within Easy Reach of Indian Passport |
| Inactive Government Firms | India Has 383 Govt-Run Firms That Do Nothing — Zilch   |

The headline should tell the whole story even if the reader reads nothing else.

**Section headers are mini-headlines, not labels.**

Not _"Remittances."_ Better: _"Gulf Rules Remittances."_
Not _"Universities."_ Better: _"Top Targets Like Harvard Face $1Bn Hit."_

**Body copy is a staircase of small revelations.** Each step takes you one floor deeper. Use rhetorical questions to create momentum — _Can such dead companies still "employ" people? What if they simply disappear?_ — then answer them immediately.

Write like a brilliantly informed friend at a coffee shop. Short sentences. Vivid verbs. Specific numbers. Confident interpretation. Tell the reader what to _infer_, not just what to _see_. "Why that matters" callouts earn their keep.

## The Story Architecture

Every flap follows this spine, even when it doesn't look like it:

1. **Hook** — the surprising claim
2. **Context** — why we're talking about this now
3. **Proof** — the strongest numbers first
4. **Expansion** — 3–5 supporting angles
5. **Human consequence** — who feels this in real life
6. **Bigger meaning** — what larger trend or warning does this point to

## The Tests That Matter

**The 5-second test:** If someone glances at this for five seconds, do they walk away knowing the main argument? If not, simplify and make the central idea bigger.

**The "feel smart" test:** These flaps are not trying to _look_ smart. They are trying to **make the reader feel smart**. That is the bar.

**The "originality" test:** If you can find the same argument in a mainstream media story, it's not original. The flap should feel like a new insight, not a rehash.
```
