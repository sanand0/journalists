Times Special

Is Japan the future of every country?

Imagine a country that, for three decades, has barely grown economically, where interest rates are stuck at zero, prices are frozen, and wages barely budge. Yet, this nation is a global economic powerhouse, maintains a high quality of life, excellent education, and remarkably low unemployment. What if this seemingly stagnant economy isn't a cautionary tale, but a glimpse into humanity's future — a world where ceaseless growth isn't the only measure of success, and where an ageing society can still thrive?

By Chandrima Banerjee

For Decades In Japan…

Three economic conditions that have defined Japan's era of stagnation — and are now starting to slowly shift.

01
Wages haven't grown
Deflation left lasting habits — workers stopped expecting raises, companies avoided offering them. It was only recently that wage negotiations led to slightly stronger pay hikes.
02
Neither has cost of living
Low interest rates usually nudge prices up by encouraging borrowing. In Japan, they could not lift inflation to the 2% target. It's only now, since 2022, that inflation is above target.
03
Or the cost of borrowing
After a real estate and stock market bubble burst, the Bank of Japan slashed rates in 1991, introduced zero interest rate policy in 1999, negative rates in 2016 — reversed only in 2024.

And, At The Same Time…

Three structural features of Japanese society that compound its economic stagnation — and mirror trends now emerging worldwide.

1. It's Ageing Really Fast

Japan's fertility rate hasn't been above replacement level since 1974. Today it stands at 1.21 — one of the world's lowest. Life expectancy is close to 85. Median age: 49.

2. People Rarely Switch Jobs

In early 2025, just 17% of Japanese workers said they wanted to change jobs. Of those, 63% weren't even looking. Fewer than 5% actually changed jobs.

Why That Matters
When people don't change jobs, wages stagnate. Job mobility is a key driver of pay growth — workers earn more by moving to employers who value their skills more highly.

3. People Save a Lot — In Cash

Of the nearly $15 trillion that Japanese households hold in financial assets, half sits in cash or deposits — a share that has actually grown since the 1990s.

Why That Matters
That much idle capital means less flows into productive investment. Savings parked in low-yield deposits dampen economic dynamism and compound the stagnation cycle.

Is This Where We Are All Headed?

UN projections show the share of countries with fertility rates below replacement level is going off a cliff — from 55% today, expected to rise to 94% by end of this century.

Like in Japan, job transitions are slow in ageing societies. Greece (52%) and Italy (48%) have higher shares of workers in jobs for 10+ years than Japan (46%).
