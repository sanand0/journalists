India has 383 govt-run firms that do nothing — zilch. So, why do they exist?

Across India, these companies drag on because they haven't been officially shut down — some haven't produced anything for decades, and some have actually gone 'missing'. Why do they still cost taxpayers money?

By Chandrima Banerjee

18%
of all public-sector units in India are inactive
383 PSUs — owned by state govts or the Centre — sit idle, based on CAG report data

How It Works

In India, govts set up and operate firms for three primary reasons — to make things more affordable, to develop infrastructure in a planned manner, and to protect strategic interests. But what happens when the firms stop doing what they were meant to do?

Bihar
The Bihar State Forest Development Corporation Limited has been lying inactive for more than 20 years. No work, no output. Bihar has 39 such inactive state-run units, with almost ₹790 crore of state money tied up.
Assam
Assam has 11 inactive companies. Three of them were 'untraceable' when the CAG asked. But they account for almost ₹12 crore in capital employed.
West Bengal
West Bengal has 19 inactive state-run firms with outstanding loans running to more than ₹1,100 crore. But together they had almost 1,500 employees.
Source: CAG audit report

Why Does Closing Them Take Years?

The CAG repeats this point over and over — inactive PSUs do not contribute to economic growth, and those investments need to be squared off fast.

  1. Govt decides to shut down (after considering revival vs closure)
  2. Process can involve courts, tribunals, and liquidators
  3. Must figure out: what to do with assets, how to pay employees' dues, how to repay loans
  4. All while the govt keeps an eye on money needed — closing a company can be quite expensive
  5. Centre said it's coming up with a plan for "champion" central PSEs

While closure guidelines were issued in 2021, actual implementation has been slow, a parliamentary panel said.

What Do Other Countries Do?

Globally, 126 of the largest 500 companies by revenue are state-owned. So, they can work. But figuring out how to wind up inactive ones is something prudent govts routinely do.

Sweden
An updated state ownership policy from 2025 requires state-owned enterprises to operate on commercial principles and make money — ensuring economic value for taxpayers.
Norway
The govt's goal as an owner is 'the highest possible return over time', with clear expectations of dividend payments.

India's challenge is that it has never demanded this of its state-run firms — not with explicit expectations anyway.
