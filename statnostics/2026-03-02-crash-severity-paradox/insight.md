# Road safety paradox

- Headline: Road safety paradox: Tamil Nadu reports the most crashes, but Uttar Pradesh records the most deaths.
- Key numbers: **Accidents (2023):** TN 67,213; MP 55,327; Kerala 48,091; UP 44,534; Karnataka 43,440\. **Fatalities (2023):** UP 23,652; TN 18,347; Maharashtra 15,366; MP 13,798; Karnataka 12,321.
- Why surprising: People assume “most accidents = most deaths,” but the leaders differ sharply. It’s a clean split-screen chart: one state tops collisions, another tops fatalities—instantly shareable and debate-sparking (enforcement? road mix? reporting?).

## The national frame (use as the “big number” header)

- **Total crashes (2023): 4,80,583**
- **Total deaths (2023): 1,72,890**
- **Total injured (2023): 4,62,825**
- **Fatal crashes:** 1,60,509 (**33.4%** of all crashes)
- **Injury crashes:** 2,92,424 (**60.8%**)
  - **Grievous injury crashes:** 1,58,576 (**33.0%**)
  - **Minor injury crashes:** 1,33,848 (**27.9%**)

**Humanizing conversion (derived from totals):**

- ≈ **1,317 crashes/day** and **474 deaths/day**
- ≈ **55 crashes/hour** and **~20 deaths/hour** (the report itself notes “~20 deaths every hour”).

## The “lethality gap” callout (this makes it viral)

Using the above crash+death counts:

- **Tamil Nadu:** ~**27.3 deaths per 100 crashes** (18,347 / 67,213) _(derived)_
- **Uttar Pradesh:** ~**53.1 deaths per 100 crashes** (23,652 / 44,534) _(derived)_

**Suggested label text:**

> “UP doesn’t have the most crashes. It has the deadliest crashes.”

## Where crashes happen (second panel)

By road type (2023):

- **National Highways:** 1,50,177 crashes; **63,112 deaths**
- **State Highways:** 1,05,662 crashes; **39,439 deaths**
- **Other roads:** 2,24,744 crashes; **70,339 deaths**

**Shares (derived from the same counts):**

- “Other roads” are **46.8% of crashes** but **40.7% of deaths**.
- NH are **31.2% of crashes** but **36.5% of deaths**.

**Interpretation caption:**

> “India’s road safety crisis isn’t only a highway story. It’s also a ‘non-highway’ story.”

## Who dies (third panel: people, not vehicles)

- **Age:** 18–45 years are **66.4%** of road-death victims; 18–60 years are **83.4%**.
- **Road user:** **Two-wheeler riders = 44.8%** of fatalities; **pedestrians = 20.4%**.

## The single biggest lever (fourth panel: cause)

- **Overspeeding:** **68.4% of crashes**, **68.1% of deaths**, **69.2% of injuries**.
- Road-death severity (persons killed per 100 crashes) **slightly decreased to 36 in 2023 from 36.5 in 2022**.

**Implications (strong, simple):**

- If you want one villain: **speed**.
- If you want one neglected group: **two-wheeler riders + pedestrians**.

**Contrarian angle (worth stating on the graphic):**

- A high-crash state can look “worse” partly because it **records and registers more consistently**, while a high-death state can reflect **higher-speed environments, delayed trauma care, riskier road mixes, or under-counted nonfatal crashes**.
